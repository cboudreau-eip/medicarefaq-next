## Lesson 1: What Is Medicare?

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/what-is-medicare/`  
**Estimated read time:** 5 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Establish the basics so the reader has a clear mental model of what Medicare is and is not.

### Key Content Points

1. **Medicare is federal health insurance for people 65+** (and certain younger people with disabilities or End-Stage Renal Disease). It is run by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS), not private insurance companies.

2. **Medicare is NOT free health insurance.** Most people pay $0 for Part A, but Part B costs $185/month in 2026. There are also deductibles, copays, and coinsurance. Many people are surprised by how much they still owe out of pocket.

3. **Medicare does NOT cover everything.** It covers about 80% of approved medical costs. The remaining 20% has no cap - meaning a $200,000 hospital bill could leave you owing $40,000. This is why supplemental coverage exists.

4. **Two paths to get Medicare coverage:**
   - Path 1: Original Medicare (Parts A + B) + a Medicare Supplement plan + Part D drug plan
   - Path 2: Medicare Advantage (Part C) - replaces Original Medicare with a private plan

5. **You do not choose between Medicare and private insurance.** Medicare IS your primary insurance after 65 (unless you have qualifying employer coverage). The choice is how you receive and supplement your Medicare benefits.

### Eddie Pro Tip
"Here is the single most important thing to understand: Medicare covers 80% of your approved medical costs. There is no annual out-of-pocket maximum on Original Medicare. That means without supplemental coverage, your financial exposure is unlimited. A serious illness or hospital stay could cost you tens of thousands of dollars. That is why almost everyone needs either a Medigap plan or a Medicare Advantage plan on top of Original Medicare."

### FAQ for this lesson
- **Q: Is Medicare the same as Medicaid?** A: No. Medicare is federal health insurance based on age (65+) or disability. Medicaid is a joint federal-state program for people with limited income and resources. Some people qualify for both (called "dual eligible").
- **Q: Do I have to sign up for Medicare at 65?** A: If you are already receiving Social Security, you will be enrolled automatically. Otherwise, you need to actively sign up. If you have employer coverage, you may be able to delay - but the rules are specific (covered in Lesson 3).
- **Q: Can I keep my employer insurance and Medicare?** A: It depends on your employer size. If your employer has 20+ employees, your employer plan is primary and you can delay Medicare. If fewer than 20, Medicare is primary. We cover this in detail in Lesson 3.

## Lesson 2: The 4 Parts of Medicare

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/parts-of-medicare/`  
**Estimated read time:** 5 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Clearly explain what each part covers so the reader stops confusing them.

### Key Content Points

**Part A - Hospital Insurance**
- Covers: Inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility (up to 100 days), hospice, some home health
- Cost: $0 premium for most people (if you or spouse paid Medicare taxes 10+ years). Deductible: $1,676 per benefit period in 2026.
- Key gap: You pay $0 for days 1-60, then $419/day for days 61-90, then $838/day for "lifetime reserve days" (60 total). After that, you pay everything.

**Part B - Medical Insurance**
- Covers: Doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, lab tests, durable medical equipment, ambulance
- Cost: $185/month standard premium in 2026. Annual deductible: $257. After deductible, you pay 20% of approved costs with no cap.
- Key gap: That 20% coinsurance has no annual limit. A $500,000 cancer treatment means $100,000 out of pocket.

**Part C - Medicare Advantage**
- Private plans that replace Original Medicare. Bundles A + B + usually D.
- Often $0 premium (beyond Part B). Includes out-of-pocket maximum ($9,250 in 2026).
- Trade-off: Network restrictions (HMO/PPO), prior authorizations, and potential claim denials.

**Part D - Prescription Drug Coverage**
- Standalone drug plans that work with Original Medicare (or built into Part C).
- Average premium: $34.50/month in 2026. Deductible: up to $615.
- Coverage phases: deductible, initial coverage, coverage gap, catastrophic.

### Comparison Table
| | Part A | Part B | Part C | Part D |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What it covers | Hospital | Medical/Doctor | All-in-one (private) | Prescriptions |
| Monthly premium | $0 (most people) | $185 | Varies ($0-$200+) | ~$34.50 avg |
| Deductible | $1,676/benefit period | $257/year | Varies by plan | Up to $615/year |
| Network | Any Medicare provider | Any Medicare provider | Plan network only | Plan formulary |

### Eddie Pro Tip
"Think of it this way: Part A is your hospital card, Part B is your doctor card, Part D is your pharmacy card, and Part C is an alternative that bundles everything into one private plan. Most of my clients do best with Original Medicare (A + B) plus a Medigap plan plus a standalone Part D plan. That combination gives you the most freedom and the most predictable costs."

### FAQ for this lesson
- **Q: Do I need all four parts?** A: You need Part A and Part B (Original Medicare). Then you choose: either add a Supplement + Part D (Path 1), or switch to Part C/Advantage (Path 2). You cannot have both a Supplement and Advantage at the same time.
- **Q: What is a "benefit period" for Part A?** A: A benefit period starts when you are admitted to the hospital and ends when you have been out for 60 consecutive days. If you go back to the hospital after 60 days, a new benefit period starts and you pay the deductible again.

## Lesson 3: Enrollment Windows and Deadlines

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/enrollment-windows/`  
**Estimated read time:** 5 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Make enrollment timing crystal clear so the reader does not accidentally miss a deadline and face penalties.

### Key Content Points

**Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) - The Most Important Window**
- 7-month window: 3 months before your 65th birthday month, your birthday month, and 3 months after.
- This is when you sign up for Part A and Part B.
- This is also your one guaranteed-issue window for Medigap (no health questions, no denial).
- If you enroll in the first 3 months, coverage starts on the 1st of your birthday month.

**Medigap Open Enrollment Period (OEP)**
- 6-month window starting when you are both 65+ AND enrolled in Part B.
- During this window, no insurance company can deny you a Medigap policy or charge you more due to health conditions.
- After this window closes, carriers can (and will) use medical underwriting. Pre-existing conditions can mean denial.
- This is the single most important enrollment window for Medigap.

**Special Enrollment Period (SEP)**
- Triggered by life events: losing employer coverage, moving, losing Medicaid.
- For Part B: 8-month SEP after employer coverage ends (no penalty).
- For Medigap: Guaranteed issue rights exist in specific situations (losing employer coverage, plan leaves your area, etc.) but are more limited than the OEP.

**Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) - October 15 to December 7**
- For changing Medicare Advantage plans or Part D plans.
- NOT for Medigap (Medigap can be changed anytime, but underwriting applies outside OEP).

**Late Enrollment Penalties**
- Part B: 10% premium increase for each 12-month period you delayed without qualifying coverage. This is permanent.
- Part D: 1% of the national base premium for each month you delayed. Also permanent.

### Eddie Pro Tip
"The number one mistake I see: people turning 65 who are still working assume they can sign up for Medigap whenever they want. Wrong. Your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period is a use-it-or-lose-it window. If you wait until you are 68 to retire and then try to get a Medigap plan, you will face medical underwriting. If you have had a heart attack, cancer, or diabetes, you may be denied entirely. Even if you delay Part B because of employer coverage, talk to a licensed agent BEFORE your 65th birthday to understand your timeline."

### FAQ for this lesson
- **Q: I am still working at 65 with employer coverage. What do I do?** A: If your employer has 20+ employees, your employer plan is primary. You can delay Part B without penalty. When you retire (or lose that coverage), you get an 8-month SEP to enroll in Part B and a 6-month Medigap OEP. But confirm with your HR department and a Medicare agent - the rules are specific.
- **Q: What if I missed my Medigap OEP?** A: You can still apply for Medigap, but carriers can ask health questions and may deny you. Some states have birthday rules or guaranteed issue protections (check your state page). If you are healthy, you may still qualify. If you have health conditions, Medicare Advantage may be your best alternative.
- **Q: Can I change my Medigap plan later?** A: Yes, you can apply to switch anytime, but you will face medical underwriting (unless your state has a birthday rule or you have guaranteed issue rights). This is why choosing the right plan during your OEP is so important.

## Lesson 4: The Coverage Gap - What Medicare Does NOT Cover

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/coverage-gaps/`  
**Estimated read time:** 5 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Create urgency by showing exactly what Original Medicare leaves uncovered, making the case for supplemental coverage.

### Key Content Points

**The 20% Problem**
- After your Part B deductible ($257), Medicare pays 80% of approved charges. You pay 20%.
- There is NO annual out-of-pocket maximum on Original Medicare.
- Real-world example: A hip replacement costs $40,000. Medicare pays $32,000. You owe $8,000.
- Worst case: A cancer treatment costing $300,000 means $60,000 out of pocket.
- This is the primary reason Medicare Supplement plans exist.

**Part A Gaps**
- Hospital stays beyond 60 days: $419/day for days 61-90, $838/day for lifetime reserve days.
- Skilled nursing: $0 for days 1-20, $209.50/day for days 21-100, 100% after day 100.
- Blood: First 3 pints not covered.

**Services Medicare Does NOT Cover At All**
- Most dental care (cleanings, fillings, dentures)
- Routine vision (eye exams for glasses, frames, lenses)
- Hearing aids and hearing exams for fitting
- Long-term care (nursing home custodial care)
- Cosmetic surgery
- Care outside the United States (with limited exceptions)
- Acupuncture (except for chronic low back pain)

**The Financial Risk Without Supplemental Coverage**
- Present a scenario table showing costs with and without a Medigap plan:

| Scenario | Cost Without Medigap | Cost With Plan G |
|----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 5-day hospital stay | $1,676 (Part A deductible) | $0 |
| Outpatient surgery ($15,000) | $3,000 (20% coinsurance) | $0 |
| Cancer treatment ($200,000) | $40,000+ (20% coinsurance) | $0 |
| 90-day hospital stay | $1,676 + $12,570 (days 61-90) | $0 |
| Annual total (healthy year) | $257 deductible + copays | $257 Part B deductible only |

### Eddie Pro Tip
"I have been in this business for over 20 years, and the most common regret I hear is: 'I did not think I needed a Supplement because I felt healthy.' The problem is, you do not buy insurance for when you are healthy. You buy it for the unexpected. A single hospital stay or cancer diagnosis without supplemental coverage can wipe out years of retirement savings. Plan G costs roughly $150-200/month in most states. That is your insurance against a $50,000 or $100,000 medical bill."

### FAQ for this lesson
- **Q: Does Medicare Advantage solve the coverage gap?** A: Partially. Advantage plans have an annual out-of-pocket maximum ($9,250 in 2026), which limits your exposure. However, you still pay copays and coinsurance at every visit, and you are limited to the plan's network. Medigap eliminates nearly all out-of-pocket costs with Original Medicare.
- **Q: What about dental and vision?** A: Original Medicare does not cover routine dental or vision. Medicare Advantage plans often include basic dental and vision. If you choose Original Medicare + Medigap, you would need a separate dental/vision plan (typically $30-60/month).
- **Q: Is long-term care covered by any Medicare option?** A: No. Neither Original Medicare, Medigap, nor Medicare Advantage covers long-term custodial care (nursing home stays for daily living assistance). You need a separate long-term care insurance policy for that.

## Lesson 5: Medicare Supplement vs. Medicare Advantage

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/medigap-vs-advantage/`  
**Estimated read time:** 6 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Help the reader understand the two paths and why Medigap tends to be the stronger long-term choice for most people - without being dismissive of Advantage.

### Key Content Points

**The Two Paths (Recap)**
- Path 1: Original Medicare + Medigap + Part D = maximum freedom, predictable costs, higher premium
- Path 2: Medicare Advantage (Part C) = bundled benefits, lower premium, network restrictions, cost-sharing at point of service

**Why Medigap Tends to Be the Better Long-Term Choice**

1. **No network restrictions.** With Medigap, you can see any doctor or specialist in the country who accepts Medicare. No referrals, no prior authorizations, no surprise out-of-network bills. This matters most when you are sick and need the best specialist.

2. **Predictable costs.** With Plan G, your only out-of-pocket cost is the $257 Part B deductible per year. Everything else is covered. With Advantage, you pay copays at every visit, coinsurance for procedures, and potentially thousands before hitting your out-of-pocket max.

3. **No claim denials or prior authorizations.** Medicare Advantage plans can (and do) deny claims and require prior authorization for procedures. Original Medicare with Medigap does not have this issue - if Medicare approves the service, your Supplement pays its share automatically.

4. **Portability.** Medigap works nationwide. If you travel, move, or snowbird between states, your coverage follows you. Advantage plans are typically tied to a service area.

5. **Stability as you age.** Medigap premiums increase gradually (typically 3-6% per year). The coverage never changes. Advantage plans can change benefits, networks, copays, and even leave your area every year.

**When Medicare Advantage May Make Sense**
- You are healthy, on a tight budget, and comfortable with network restrictions
- You want dental, vision, and hearing included without separate policies
- You live in an area with strong Advantage plan networks
- You understand you can switch back to Original Medicare later (but may not qualify for Medigap without underwriting)

**The "Advantage Trap" - What Most People Do Not Realize**
- You can switch FROM Medigap TO Advantage at any time during AEP.
- Switching FROM Advantage BACK TO Original Medicare + Medigap is much harder. After your Medigap OEP, you face medical underwriting. If you have developed health conditions while on Advantage, you may be denied Medigap entirely.
- This is a one-way door for many people. Choose carefully.

### Comparison Table
| Factor | Medicare Supplement (Medigap) | Medicare Advantage |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| Monthly cost | $130-250/mo (varies by state/age) | Often $0 (beyond Part B) |
| Out-of-pocket per year | ~$257 (Plan G) | Up to $9,250 |
| Doctor choice | Any Medicare doctor nationwide | Plan network only |
| Prior authorization | None | Common |
| Claim denials | Rare | More common |
| Travel coverage | Nationwide + some foreign | Service area only |
| Switching flexibility | Can always switch to Advantage | Hard to switch back to Medigap |
| Best for | Long-term stability, freedom | Budget-conscious, healthy |

### Eddie Pro Tip
"Here is what I tell every client: if you can afford the Medigap premium, it is almost always the better long-term decision. I have seen too many people choose a $0 Advantage plan at 65 because they felt healthy, then develop cancer or heart disease at 72 and realize they are stuck. They cannot get back to Medigap because no carrier will accept them. The Medigap premium is not a cost - it is insurance against being trapped in a plan that does not serve you when you need it most."

### FAQ for this lesson
- **Q: Can I have both Medigap and Medicare Advantage?** A: No. It is one or the other. If you have a Medigap plan and enroll in Advantage, your Medigap policy is essentially useless (it will not pay claims). You would need to cancel it.
- **Q: What if I cannot afford Medigap?** A: Look at Plan N (typically $40-60/month less than Plan G) or High-Deductible Plan G. If those are still too expensive, Medicare Advantage with a PPO network is a reasonable alternative. The key is having SOME supplemental coverage.
- **Q: Can I switch Medigap carriers later to get a lower rate?** A: Yes, you can apply to switch carriers anytime. If you are healthy, you will likely be approved. Some states also have birthday rules that guarantee your right to switch annually. We cover this in Lesson 6.

## Lesson 6: How to Choose a Medigap Plan

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/choosing-a-medigap-plan/`  
**Estimated read time:** 6 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Give the reader a clear framework for choosing between Plan G and Plan N, and for evaluating carriers.

### Key Content Points

**The Plan Letter Decision: Plan G vs Plan N**

Plan G and Plan N are the two most popular Medigap plans for new enrollees in 2026. Here is how they differ:

| Feature | Plan G | Plan N |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Part B deductible ($257/yr) | NOT covered (you pay it) | NOT covered (you pay it) |
| Part B coinsurance (20%) | Covered 100% | Covered (with small copays) |
| Part B excess charges | Covered | NOT covered |
| Office visit copay | $0 | Up to $20 |
| ER copay (not admitted) | $0 | Up to $50 |
| Monthly premium difference | Higher | $40-60/month less |
| Best for | People who want zero surprises | Healthy people comfortable with small copays |

**The Math on Plan G vs Plan N:**
- Plan N saves roughly $40-60/month ($480-720/year) in premiums.
- Plan N costs you: up to $20 per office visit + up to $50 per ER visit (if not admitted) + potential excess charges.
- If you see the doctor 6 times per year: 6 x $20 = $120 in copays.
- Net savings with Plan N: approximately $360-600/year for most people.
- If you rarely go to the doctor, Plan N saves money. If you have frequent visits or want zero hassle, Plan G is simpler.

**How to Evaluate Carriers (The 5 Factors)**

1. **Premium price** - Get quotes from at least 3-5 carriers. Premiums for the same Plan G can vary by $50-100/month between carriers in the same ZIP code.

2. **Rate increase history** - A carrier with a low introductory rate but 15% annual increases is worse than one with a moderate rate and 3-4% increases. Ask for the carrier's rate increase history over the past 5 years.

3. **AM Best financial rating** - Look for A- or higher. This measures the carrier's ability to pay claims long-term. A+ or A++ is ideal.

4. **NAIC complaint ratio** - Below 1.0 means fewer complaints than the national average. Below 0.5 is excellent. This measures customer satisfaction and claims handling.

5. **Household/multi-policy discounts** - Many carriers offer 5-12% discounts if both spouses enroll or if you bundle with other policies.

**Pricing Methods (How Carriers Set Rates)**
- **Attained-age:** Premium increases as you age (most common). Starts low, rises over time.
- **Issue-age:** Premium based on your age when you buy. Does not increase due to aging (still increases for inflation/medical costs).
- **Community-rated:** Same premium regardless of age. Higher starting premium but more predictable long-term.

### Eddie Pro Tip
"My recommendation for most new enrollees: start with Plan G. Yes, Plan N can save you money, but Plan G eliminates all decision-making. You never wonder if a visit will cost you a copay, you never worry about excess charges, and you never have to think about whether to go to the ER. That peace of mind is worth $40-60/month to most people. If budget is tight, Plan N is an excellent plan - just understand the trade-offs."

### FAQ for this lesson
- **Q: Why not Plan F?** A: Plan F is only available to people who became eligible for Medicare before January 1, 2020. If you turned 65 after that date, Plan G is your best option (it is identical to Plan F except you pay the $257 Part B deductible).
- **Q: How do I find the cheapest Plan G in my state?** A: Use our state-specific guides (linked below) or enter your ZIP code to get personalized quotes. Rates vary significantly by ZIP code, age, gender, and tobacco use.
- **Q: Can I switch from Plan N to Plan G later?** A: You can apply, but you will face medical underwriting. If you have developed health conditions, you may be denied. This is why many agents recommend starting with Plan G - you can always switch down to Plan N later (easier to go from more coverage to less).

## Lesson 7: Your Next Steps

**Slug:** `/medicare-101-course/next-steps/`  
**Estimated read time:** 4 minutes  
**Lesson goal:** Convert the educated reader into a lead. Clear, specific action steps with strong CTAs.

### Key Content Points

**Your Action Plan Based on Where You Are:**

**If you are turning 65 in the next 6 months:**
1. Confirm your Part A and Part B enrollment dates (sign up at ssa.gov or your local Social Security office 3 months before your birthday).
2. Start comparing Medigap plans NOW. Your 6-month OEP starts when Part B begins.
3. Get quotes from multiple carriers in your ZIP code.
4. Choose a Part D drug plan that covers your medications.
5. Cancel or coordinate with any existing coverage (employer, marketplace, etc.).

**If you are already on Medicare but have no supplemental coverage:**
1. Understand that you may face medical underwriting for Medigap.
2. Check if your state has a birthday rule or other guaranteed issue protections.
3. Consider Medicare Advantage as an alternative if you cannot qualify for Medigap.
4. Talk to a licensed agent who can assess your options based on your health history.

**If you are on Medicare Advantage and considering a switch:**
1. Know that you can switch to Original Medicare during the Annual Enrollment Period (Oct 15 - Dec 7) or the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (Jan 1 - Mar 31).
2. You will need to apply for Medigap separately - and underwriting applies.
3. If you are healthy, you may qualify. If you have conditions, talk to an agent first.
4. You will also need to enroll in a standalone Part D plan.

**Why Work with a Licensed Agent (Not a Call Center)**
- Licensed agents represent multiple carriers (not just one).
- They can compare rates across all available carriers in your ZIP code.
- There is no cost to you - agents are compensated by the carrier, not the client.
- They can help you navigate underwriting if you are outside your OEP.
- They provide ongoing service (annual reviews, rate shopping, claims help).
